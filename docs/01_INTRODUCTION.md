# Introduction

## Background

Poxviruses are large, double-stranded DNA viruses that carry their own transcriptional machinery and replicate entirely in the cytoplasm of the host cell. Vaccinia virus (VACV) is one of the most prototypical poxviruses and it is the most intensively studied model within the family. VACV is closely related to the agents of both smallpox (variola) and mpox (monkeypox); therefore, this makes the biology directly relevant to human disease and further to smallpox/mpox vaccines which, in of themselves, are poxvirus-based. 

One of the defining features of a poxvirus infection is a process called “host shutoff”. This process is one in which the virus broadly suppresses expression of the host gene and further the protein synthesis of the host gene while sustaining efficient translation of its own mRNAs. This reprogramming from the virus is both dynamic and tightly timed. The genes of the virus are expressed in the early, intermediate, and even late waves whereas the response from the host unfolds post-infection over hours. Therefore, capturing *which* host genes and *which* pathways change, and *when* they do so, is central to understanding the ways in which poxviruses commandeer the cell. 

## The Transcriptome and Why Time Matters

The transcriptome is a compilation of the complete set of RNA transcripts (mRNAs) that are expressed in a given moment in a cell. This is measured through a process called RNA sequencing (also referred to as RNA-seq) before and during an infection. This would ideally be completed across a time course, for example 2 hours, 6 hours, and 24 hours post-infection. Through its measuring, increases and decreases in gene expression can be identified that are potential candidates for host responses. Dynamic temporal control is essential for a successful infection and therefore, time-resolved transcriptomics is the natural lens to look through for this biology. 

## The Literature Landscape & the Gap

There are studies that currently exist for poxvirus transcriptome, however they mainly cluster into one of the two following groups:

- ++Viral-Side / Methodological Studies++: this cluster is one that maps the own transcriptome of the virus with fine detail. These studies often use long-read sequencing (e.g., Tombácz et al. 2021, long-read full-length VACV time course) and 2018 GigaScience long-read data descriptor. This cluster characterizes viral kinetic classes, sites of transcription start/end, and even isoforms. However, this cluster quantifies little of the host response. 
- ++Host-Side Studies++: This cluster is one for infection response and this is sparser. Some notable examples of this include Yang & Moss 2015 (RNA-seq plus ribosome profiling of HeLa that is VACV-infection across a time course), Park et al. 2025 (Nature Microbiology and is RACK1/elF3-centered non-canonical initiation of translation during shutoff), and also Matía et al. 2024 preprint (single-cell transcriptomics + proteomics and these report conserved host changes across cell contexts).

Notably, work existing between the intersection of poxviruses and host RNA helicases is very limited. A Myxoma virus screen implicating DHX29/DHX35 is one of the few examples. Furthermore, while there are individual host-response datasets publicly available online, they were generated by different labs, they were generated in different cell types, and further they were generated with different designs. No integrated, reproducibility-focused synthesis of which of the host genes are being constantly remodeled across these various independent studies has yet to be assembled. 

## What This Project Does

PoxHostAtlas will address this aforementioned gap with a meta-analysis of reproducible public-data. This project will systematically gather the independent public poxvirus host-response datasets and further this project will harmonize the differential expression and ultimately ask: in an unbiased and genome-wide way, which host genes and pathways are remodeled reproducibly across labs, viruses, and cell types, and further which host hubs are targeted the most? This project will be a secondary analysis resource for hypothesis generation and this project is not claiming the primary discovery of the underlying datasets or mechanistic proof.
